Risk Threshold Database Objective

The Risk Theshold Database (RTD) compilies climate parameter levels whose excedence would increase the likelihood of damages and losses (D&Ls) associated with a given asset, investment, or locality. These can be applied to climate change screening of public assets, investments, and sub-national governments using the Geospatial Planning and Budgeting Platform (GPBP).

FRAMEWORK

Climate Parameters

Different climate parameters - such as heat, cold, rain, drought, wind, as well as climate related hazards such as floods and wild filers - can increase the risk of damages and losses (D&L) associated with adverse impacts on service delivery or revenues associated public assets. Damages reduces stocks (e.g., a heavily damaged or destroyed school), which losses reduce flows of goods and services (e.g., generation of education services). Adaptation costs in the context of public asset and investment management would refer to the additional expenses required for interventions to reduce prospective damages and losses. Cost benefit analysis (CBA) aims at a project or asset level aims to quantify in monetary terms which decisions to make and which ones to forgo.

Hazard and Exposure

The risk of future D&Ls will depend on the asset and associated activity in question (e.g., transport services associated with bridges, education with schools), and the probabilities that excedance of particular climate parameters over any period would result with a disruption/D&L. Both hazard and exposure parameters are suject to uncertainty in terms of quantifying risk. However, different data sources increasilgly providing a basis for more data-informed decision making.

Fragility Curves

Fragility curves provide for one framework to capture the possible risk profiles of a single or portfolio of non-financial assets and/or investments. The intuition of these curves is that higher exposure levels to a given hazards – e.g., peak gust wind speed (PGWS) due to hurricane – is more likely to result in damages and losses. Figure 1 provides for a stylized fragility curve for extreme temperature (e.g., daily maximum exceeded or heat wave), for example of education delivery in an un-airconditioned school. The likelihood that learning will be impaired grows at higher temperature exposures. At over 40 degrees, this is likely to disrupt half of school operations for at least one day per year. Higher or more protracted temperature extremes may damage structures and increase losses. Curves may be placed to reflect some sets of minor, moderate, severe distributions, as well as full-fledged disruption (e.g., for maximum wind gust illustration).

Resources

The RTD entries are typically associated with one or more Nomenclature of Economic Actiivties (NACE) codes at 3 or 4 digital code level classifications. NACE is the Statistical Classification of Economic Activities in the European Community. These may be derived from empirical studies (e.g., WTW 2022), construction standard ratings (e.g., Eurocodes), as well as structural simulations (e.g., digital twin techniques).

The Risk Thresholds are formulated in two main ways:

Risk is calculated based the aggregate frequency/duration (in days) within a given unit of time (e.g. one year) that a given Hazard Criticality Threshold is exceeded. Thresholds may be tailored by the user taking account of local risk tolerance or assumed levels of resilience of a given asset type.

Each RTD entry may assume from 2-4 thresholds. The exceedance of the highest one if applied in the GPBP CCC would trigger a red classification for that climate parameter (e.g., heat, cold, rain, wind, etc.).
